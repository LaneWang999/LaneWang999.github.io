Bergson, Henri. Laughter: an essay on the meaning of the comic. Translated by Cloudesley Brereton and Fred Rothwell. New York: The Macmillan Company, 1911.

★★★★☆
I started my research from exploring how humour works in art and design. I saw humour as a prominent part of my practice, but I gradually lost interest in over-analysing one single element and gradually shifted focus. That said, this essay remains valuable, especially in the way it links humour with humanity through a fundamental, philosophical lens. It also proposes that laughter exists in the space between life and art, which is where my own observations take place. He also asserts that the core of a joke is the suggestion—an implicit response to a specific mental or environmental state, which supports how visual cues often function in art and design.
x
Heller, Steven. Graphic wit : the art of humor in design, New York: Watson-Guptill, 1991.

★★★☆☆
This book provides a comprehensive study of humour in graphic design by breaking it down to various types and examining multiple cases. It provides useful insights to how humour can help design works transcend basic communication and provoke subsequent reactions and reflections, which can also make the communication more memorable and effective. The interviews with designers such as Paul Rand and Paula Scher address some of my main concerns regarding humour in graphic design: whether the humour is intentional, whether it is about content or delivery, and where the stance of the audience is.
x
Selkirk, Neil. “TIBOR KALMAN'S USE OF PHOTOGRAPHY.” Aperture, no. 159, 2000. http://www.jstor.org/stable/24472622.

★★★☆☆
This article analyses how photography serves as a communication tool in Kalman's work, where the designer often incorporates images from everyday life and “low” culture, not only for their aesthetics but also for their ability to carry strong cultural and social messages. The author discusses the cultural background and significance of visual signs, along with the history of using found stock photographs for storytelling.
x
Steyerl, Hito. "In Defence of the Poor Image." e-flux. Published November 2009. http://worker01.e-flux.com/pdf/article_94.pdf

★★★★★
This article articulates clearly how the poor image is both a result of and a response to its own reality. This is reflected in its dual nature: it is both a product of capitalism and a form of resistance against it. Unlike the high-resolution image which is concerned with originality and ownership, the poor image is about reality, at the expense of image quality. Understanding the poor image has deep significance for me as someone grown up surrounded by pirated moving images. It also expresses clearly the position from which I create and why I'm drawn to the poor image: one that values accessibility over authority, lived experience over quality. The poor quality is never a weakness; it's what makes it relevant. And this relevance is central to my practice.
x
Lionis, Chrisoula. Comedy in Crises, Weaponising Humour in Contemporary Art. Palgrave Macmillan Cham, 2023.

★★★☆☆
This book offers a fresh understanding of both the creation and reception of humour in contemporary art. It asserts that humour in art has a transformative power, especially in context of global crisis and difficult socio-political situations. This resonates with my interest in how humour can serve as a strong tool for emotional and conceptual communication. More importantly, it examines humour in real-world societal context, and provides insight into how humour is weaponised in contemporary art to address complex cultural identities, as well as its role in strengthening cultural resilience.
x
Berlant, Lauren, and Sianne Ngai. “Comedy Has Issues.” Critical Inquiry 43, no. 2 (2017): 233-249. https://doi.org/10.1086/689666.

★★★☆☆
The article offers a dense critical analysis of the complex and often contradictory nature of comedy. It explores its inherent tension between pleasure and discomfort, acceptance and critique. Through close reading of a racist joke, the author demonstrates that while judgment often lands on the intention behind the joke, its reception is equally crucial. In this way, social norms are both subverted and reinforced.
x
Lupton, Ellen. "High and low: A strange case of us and them." Eye 7, no. 2, 1992.

★★★★★
This is the article that drew my attention to the cultural hierarchy within the vernacular. It points out that the relationship between high and low culture is not a matter of content but of structure by examining how vernaculars are perceived historically in graphic design.
x
Venturi, Robert, Steven Izenour, and Denise Scott Brown. Learning from Las Vegas: The Forgotten Symbolism of Architectural Form. Cambridge, MA: The MIT Press, 1977.

★★★★☆
This book makes a strong argument about how everyday, “ugly” and even vulgar buildings should be included in the discourse of design studies, challenging the principles of traditional modernist design. Although written in the context of architecture, the main kinds of buildings argued in this book, the decorative sheds and duck buildings, function more through shapes and signs instead of architectural structure.

Vernacular Photography
Take 1

I had a hard time articulating the kinds of images I pursue in my practice, and one of the closest terms I've come across in my research is vernacular, which I hated at first sight even though it fits: it's usually low-quality, raw, improvised, unaltered and practical. I used to think the word “vernacular" stood against what I believe in by imposing a hierarchy on perfectly natural, everyday and undefined subjects. But I've come to realise it does make sense since there's never a unified "us", and I have actually been separating myself from the industry and my work from the mainstream for years. The vernacular wanders on and off the margin of the so-called "art scene", and it's important for them not to conform to the clear, clean, polished, and pretty images.

Xiaolonghua. Untitled. 2024. http://xhslink.com/a/1iQZ4uym0BMbb.
In Xiaolonghua's Untitled, the local greasy spoon diner composes their menu as the teeth of an upbeat smiley face.

Vernacular Photography
Take 2

In vernacular photography, content is usually more important than the form it appears in, and sometimes even the form should be viewed as content. This is because vernacular photography, compared to non-vernacular photography, is technically closer to an object than an abstraction. For instance, in Chi Li's Morning, the tiles are distorted in the reflection of a stainless-steel desk piled with packaged fried tofu. We can imagine that it was the distortion that caught the photographer's eye, but it's never only about the abstracted lines and shapes. This non-rhetorical characteristic is not usually the case in contemporary art and design.

Li, Chi. [Morning]. 2020. https://m.weibo.cn/status/4561714899325594?wm=3333_2001&from=10F4393010&sourcetype=weixin&featurecode=newtitle&s_trans=1874642752_4561714899325594&s_channel=4&jumpfrom=weibocom.

Vernacular photography
Take 3

There's a kind of solidity within the fluid structure of vernacular photography. Extra information like captions or titles are completely optional. For example, happy-customer-81.jpg illustrates a customer who happily poses with his newly installed bathtub. Without the caption, it would be a weird scene of a man standing beside a bathtub, with a faint smile on his face and a window with blinding daylight beside his head, which is a different interpretation but also not wrong.

hry grfn. happy-customer-81.jpg. 2019. Are.na. https://www.are.na/block/5051858.

Vernacular Photography
Take 4

Juxtaposition is a great way to present vernacular photography, since the relationship between the two images overpowers other information. In Fisher & Sons Funeral Home, a photo of the residence from a popular HBO series taken by a fan is placed next to a still from the show. Positioning them side by side reveals more subtle information as viewers can easily study the differences and similarities between the two images.

Lynch, Michael. Fisher & Sons Funeral Home. 2024. Facebook, public group “Film Locations - Then and Now.” https://www.facebook.com/groups/3164534807204454/posts/3957692847888642/.

Vernacular Photography
Take 5

For me, the charm of vernacular photography lies in how it can be accepted just as it is, while also free of interpretation. The meaning of vernacular photography is always fluid. The standpoint of vernacular is not about originality but about relevance. Personal experience is counted and ambiguity is accepted. This reveals how some of our most personal experiences become universal and starts to form a culture of our own. In the Family photo from a series of found negatives, the content is so personal and specific that almost only those with a similar background or experience might have the potential to understand it. But still, we can recognise some information about the image intuitively.

Unknown photographer. Family photo (discarded negative). n.d. Found and developed by Yajiang Dong, 2019. https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/LwwWvKorSDaOlVTTG1jgxw.

Found Object
Take 1

“found” can imply both a condition and an action, describing either the materiality of the object, or the act of its discovery. Some found objects are actually lost at some point. They are usually not recontextualised intentionally, but are preserved or documented in the state they were discovered. Found Object: May 25, 2020 from Michelle Lopez illustrates an extremely expressive scene of an explosion. There is no one present in the image, but we can strongly feel their presence through the almost brutally straightforward trace of evidence. Two identities co-exist within the object: the object itself and the perspective of the finder.

Lopez, Michelle. Found Object: May 25, 2020. 2020. Giclée print, ed. 2/20, 11 x 8 1/2 in. Permanent Collection. Pennsylvania Academy of the Fine Arts. https://www.jstor.org/stable/community.38267737.
x
Found Object
Take 3

Found objects can remain physically unaltered, which has strong power as it challenges conventional notions of skill, authorship, and aesthetics. Originated in art history as readymades, these objects are artifacts unmodified but recontextualised. They remain unchanged but are placed directly into new settings. There is usually a juxtaposition between the artwork and the context, and the artwork relies on the tension between the two. For example, in Ceal Floyer's Do Not Remove, the original meaning of the sign now applies to the world of art, revealing the ironic dynamic of authority within gallery settings.

Floyer, Ceal. Do Not Remove. 2011. Wall plugs, ready-made “Do Not Remove” sign, wall, dimensions variable. https://jstor.org/stable/community.10824932.

Graphic Design
Take 1

As I began studying graphic design, I felt a huge gap between the look of a work and the thinking behind it. Designs come from ideas, and convey the ideas through form. There is always a rationale, which often has little to do with what the viewers actually see. I find that being able to tell the idea and the visual apart is helpful both as a designer and as an audience. I'm not saying the visual appearance and the underlying thinking are two completely separate processes, but they are not an entity either and some works rely more heavily on one than the other. Graphic Design
Take 2

Some designs are built on ideas. The idea and its execution are often extremely simple and interconnected. Every design choice made in the work serves the idea. The idea for An invitation for ROOM is to write a typical adolescent rejection letter because the sender needs to attend the ROOM preview. It then incorporates the typical handwritten letters from teenage girls at that time, with big circles dotting the i's and j's.


Mooren, Maureen and Daniel van der Velden. An invitation for ROOM. n.d. In ed. Ellen Lupton. Graphic Design Thinking : Beyond Brainstorming, 170. Princeton Architectural Press, 2011.

Graphic Design
Take 3

Sometimes the idea itself is a visual idea. For example, Tibor Kalman's Talking Heads logo turns the two As in the band name on their heads, making them look strange while still balanced and legible.


Kalman, Tibor. A logo for Talking Heads. n.d. In Liz Farrelly. Tibor Kalman: Design and Undesign, 9. New York: Watson-Guptill Publications, 1998.

Graphic Design
Take 4

Some design functions almost entirely through the visual. The idea is born during the process of typography. In David Carson's interpretation of Brian Eno, there is nothing other than a large white space, a distorted image, and fractured characters. The composition of the visual elements is contemplative and inviting, which speaks to the gentle tones and open soundscape of Eno's ambient music. There is no way to make something like this by thinking ahead. Even if there is an idea, it can't stand on its own without actual realisation. It is the visual composition that defines it.


Carson, David. Brian Eno. 1994. In Lewis Blackwell. The End of Print: The Graphic Design of David Carson. Laurence King, 2000.

Gill, Bob. Forget all the rules you ever learned about graphic design, Including the ones in this book. New York: Watson-Guptill Publications, 1981.

★★★★★
Bob Gill's work is a great example of witty and vernacular-inspired graphic design. It is clear that his work draws directly from everyday life: the visual puns and unexpected juxtapositions don't come across as pretentious or imposed, but rather light-hearted and natural. His work shows how graphic design can be inspired by everyday life and become an organic part of it, without being preoccupied by existing aesthetics and conceptions.
x
Blackwell, Lewis. The End of Print: The Graphic Design of David Carson. London: Laurence King, 2000.

★★★★★
Carson's work showcases both light-weight typographical play and serious content interpretation, often through a somehow vernacular use of type. The rich layers of the visual happenstances in life mirror his approach to creating visual compositions that communicate without rationality but speak louder than legible characters. Seemingly haphazard, the transpositions, tight croppings and incidents in his work are fine examples of design intention. It's also able to reveal the cyclical process of appreciation and creation that continuously shapes our world and becomes part of the culture itself.

Fluxus is an international, interdisciplinary network started in the early 1960s across the US, Europe, and Japan, blending art, music, design, and performance. This website provides an overview of unconventional publications central to the movement. This is a digital almanac collecting fragments I've found valuable in my design research. After 5 years in contemporary art, I've developed a practice based on real-life connections and on-site materials, and I now turn to design to better understand my work. I'm looking for more possibilities in the use of images and in ways of thinking. A key part of my practice is everyday images that prioritise content over quality. I constantly find myself running away from rhetoric and turning to the rough, unpolished images. I like to take things literally as I believe we don't do this enough. I want to figure out why I'm obsessed with this, and be able to articulate its context, aesthetic, and cultural significance. Is it because it reacts to the stale formulas of contemporary art, addresses cultural detachment, or is it because it's a form of resistance, a different source of knowledge? However, my research is a bit scattered, shooting in the dark and circling around relevant topics. I wasn't sure where to begin, so I ended up following different threads. I started by investigating the humour element in my work, then moved into the cultural hierarchy behind it, and looked more closely into photography, found objects and graphic design. I edited and rewrote the previous 2 assignments for the almanac and added some other valuable resources. The almanac takes the form of an image, visualising and reorganising threads of information and my thinking process. The key terms in this almanac are:
Humour / Vernacular / Photography / Found Object / Graphic Design / Culture
